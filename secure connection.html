Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)

QKD is the most mature and commercially available quantum technology for secure communications. It uses the fundamental principles of quantum mechanics, such as the polarization of single photons, to create and distribute a secret cryptographic key between two parties (Alice and Bob). Critically, the laws of physics guarantee that any eavesdropper attempting to measure the key will inevitably disturb the quantum state, leaving an undeniable trace. This makes the security **information-theoretically perfect**, unlike classical encryption methods.

THE QUANTUM ADVANTAGE

The No-Cloning Theorem

This core quantum theorem dictates that an arbitrary unknown quantum state (like a photon's polarization used for a key bit) cannot be perfectly copied. This principle is the bedrock of QKD, as it means an eavesdropper cannot silently intercept and duplicate the quantum key without detection.

Uncertainty and Eavesdropping

The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle ensures that measuring a quantum property (like position or momentum, or in QKD, polarization) necessarily alters it. If an unauthorized party tries to intercept the photons to read the key, the act of measurement introduces errors that Alice and Bob immediately detect when they compare a subset of the transmitted key.

Preparing for the Post-Quantum Era

While QKD secures communication channels, the rise of quantum computers threatens existing digital signatures and public-key encryption (like RSA). Research in Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) focuses on developing new classical algorithms that are resistant to quantum computer attacks.
